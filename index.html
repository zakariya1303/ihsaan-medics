Who we are

“Indeed, Allah does not change the condition of a people until they change what is within themselves.” (Qur’an 13:11)

Why we exist

Muslim medical students contribute enormously to the NHS, yet many face avoidable barriers. Limited access to mentorship, uncertainty around portfolios and career planning, and environments where faith-related needs can be misunderstood or sidelined. IHSAAN Medics exists to remove these barriers through clear guidance, genuine community, and practical opportunities, so students can progress confidently without compromising their values.

What we’re changing

IHSAAN Medics supports students through structured development, sincere mentorship, and faith-grounded professionalism — creating a bridge between training and real-world progression.

Research training

Research can feel confusing at first — not because you’re incapable, but because nobody teaches the “real-world” steps clearly. This page is designed to give you a practical, ethical, and repeatable approach to audits, QIPs, systematic reviews and meta-analyses — from idea to submission.

The goal is simple: help you build competence and confidence, so your effort turns into outcomes you can evidence in your portfolio — without shortcuts, and with integrity.

Core skills

1) Choosing a good question. Strong projects usually start with clarity. A “good” question is specific, achievable in your setting, and aligned with a meaningful outcome. For audits and QIPs, this often means a well-defined standard (e.g., local guideline, NICE, trust policy), a measurable outcome, and a realistic timescale. For reviews, it means a focused clinical question and a method that’s appropriate to the level of evidence available.

2) Methods and write-up. Most projects succeed or fail based on how well the method is planned. Think through your data source, your inclusion criteria, your variables, and how you’ll present results before you begin collecting anything. A simple structure helps: background → aim → methods → results → discussion → limitations → next steps. Clear writing is not about fancy language — it’s about being precise, honest, and easy to follow.

3) Submission and publication basics. One of the most useful mindsets is to work backwards from a target. If your aim is publication, identify a realistic journal early and skim their author guidance (format, word count, figure limits, referencing style). Then design your write-up to match those expectations from day one. Pro tip: if a journal expects a certain structure (e.g., abstract headings, ethics statement, or specific reporting checklist), you’ll save huge time by aligning early rather than rewriting later.

Practical tips we teach

  • Keep it feasible: smaller, well-run projects beat large, unfinished ones.
  • Evidence early: log meetings, approvals, and outputs (posters, certificates, abstracts) as you go.
  • Make it reproducible: use simple templates for methods, data tables, and reflection.
  • Stay ethical: understand local governance early (audit vs research vs service evaluation).

Opportunities

IHSAAN Medics aims to facilitate access where possible — especially by helping students connect with clinicians, trainees, and teams who may be running ongoing work. This can include introductions to potential supervisors, signposting to projects, and building “working groups” where students can collaborate and learn together.

We can’t guarantee supervision or placement within a specific team — availability depends on local capacity and timing — but our intention is to create a structured environment where networking happens naturally through community, shared learning, and consistent opportunities.
