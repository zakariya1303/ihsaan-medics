Research training

Research can feel confusing at first ‚Äî not because you‚Äôre incapable, but because nobody teaches the ‚Äúreal-world‚Äù steps clearly. This page gives a practical, ethical, repeatable approach to audits, QIPs, systematic reviews and meta-analyses ‚Äî from idea to submission.

Core skills

1) Choosing a good question. A ‚Äúgood‚Äù question is specific, achievable in your setting, and aligned with a meaningful outcome. For audits and QIPs, this often means a well-defined standard (e.g., local guideline, NICE, trust policy), a measurable outcome, and a realistic timescale. For reviews, it means a focused clinical question and a method appropriate to the evidence available.

2) Methods and write-up. Most projects succeed or fail based on planning. Think through your data source, inclusion criteria, variables, and how you‚Äôll present results before collecting anything. A simple structure helps: background ‚Üí aim ‚Üí methods ‚Üí results ‚Üí discussion ‚Üí limitations ‚Üí next steps.

3) Submission and publication basics. Work backwards from a target. If your aim is publication, identify a realistic journal early and skim their author guidance (format, word count, figure limits, referencing style). Align early to avoid rewriting later.

Practical tips we teach

  • Keep it feasible: smaller, well-run projects beat large, unfinished ones.
  • Evidence early: log meetings, approvals, and outputs as you go.
  • Make it reproducible: use templates for methods, data tables, and reflection.
  • Stay ethical: understand local governance early (audit vs research vs service evaluation).
