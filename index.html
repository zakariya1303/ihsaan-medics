Portfolios are often one of the most challenging aspects of preparing for specialty training.

It is easy to feel overwhelmed by large volumes of advice and unclear expectations.

With a focus on clarity and practicality, our guides are built to support consistent, purposeful development over time.

Research training

Research can feel confusing at first. This is not because you are incapable, but because the practical steps are rarely taught clearly. This page gives a practical, ethical, and repeatable approach to audits, QIPs, systematic reviews, and meta analyses, from idea to submission.

Core skills

1) Choosing a good question. A “good” question is specific, achievable in your setting, and aligned with a meaningful outcome. For audits and QIPs, this often means a well-defined standard (e.g., local guideline, NICE, trust policy), a measurable outcome, and a realistic timescale. For reviews, it means a focused clinical question and a method appropriate to the evidence available.

2) Methods and write-up. Most projects succeed or fail based on planning. Think through your data source, inclusion criteria, variables, and how you’ll present results before collecting anything. A simple structure helps: background → aim → methods → results → discussion → limitations → next steps.

3) Submission and publication basics. Work backwards from a target. If your aim is publication, identify a realistic journal early and skim their author guidance (format, word count, figure limits, referencing style). Align early to avoid rewriting later.

Practical tips we teach

  • Keep it feasible: smaller, well-run projects beat large, unfinished ones.
  • Evidence early: log meetings, approvals, and outputs as you go.
  • Make it reproducible: use templates for methods, data tables, and reflection.
  • Stay ethical: understand local governance early (audit vs research vs service evaluation).
